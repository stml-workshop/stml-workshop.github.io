Scaling Trustworthy Machine Learning (STML)

Workshop @ ICML 2023

About

The last decade of machine learning has been driven by scale—both in terms of the size of the models and the datasets they are trained on. More recently, however, this scale is reaching a new level. Indeed, “traditional” datasets such as ImageNet and the corresponding models now appear fairly small compared to the datasets and models driving machine learning performance currently. For instance, CLIP models are trained on billions of image-caption pairs and GPT-3 is trained on hundreds of billions of tokens from Common Crawl. However, despite (and, in fact, because of) their scale these modern datasets remain very much noisy, mislabeled, and biased-making the corresponding models fail in a variety of ways. Our workshop aims to bring together researchers and practitioners to develop new tools capable of coping with these challenges. Some of the challenges of focus here will be:

  • Debugging datasets: How do we efficiently identify biases in the data collection pipeline when most of the data has not been (and cannot be) manually inspected by humans? How do we identify problematic subpopulations automatically without pre-existing metadata? The extremely large scale of new datasets means that we can no longer treat them as a monolith-we have to understand their constituents in fine-grained ways.
  • Building datasets: How do we condense the vast scale of data on the internet into manageable datasets to train our models on? What are the best practices for filtering and updating datasets?
  • Understanding model behavior: How do we understand the origins of model behavior, particularly from the perspective of the data that models are trained on? How do we isolate the impact of including different data sources? This problem is already difficult for smaller models and small datasets, so making these methods scalable in modern settings such as large language models will require new techniques.
  • Training on private or untrusted data: How do we detect and prevent memorization of private data? How can we guard against adversarially manipulated data (e.g., backdoors) at these scales?
  • Understanding tradeoffs of scale: How do we leverage the benefits of large scale data without compromising our models? Is there a tradeoff between the robustness of a ML system and more data? How are scaling laws impacted by design choices?
